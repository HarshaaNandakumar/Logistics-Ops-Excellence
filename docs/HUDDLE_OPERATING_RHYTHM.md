# Huddle Operating Rhythm

How to run a 15-minute daily team huddle that doesn't waste everyone's morning.

Most daily standups drift into 30-minute status meetings that nobody wants to attend. This guide is about keeping the huddle useful — which is what makes it sustainable, which is what makes it actually change how the branch operates.

---

## The basic format

**Duration:** 15 minutes, hard cap.
**Time:** Same time every day. Most branches use 9:30 AM — late enough that overnight emails have been triaged, early enough that the day's work hasn't started.
**Attendees:** Operations team + KAM + branch manager. Optional: sales if they have skin in the day's shipments.
**Format:** Standing up, in person ideally, around a screen showing yesterday's Master sheet and today's MoM template.

## The five-minute rule per desk

Each operations desk gets roughly two minutes. They cover:

1. **How many files they closed yesterday** — goes into the Master sheet count
2. **DO releases pending or achieved** — cargo waiting for customs clearance is the most common blocker in freight ops
3. **Any blockers or escalations** — anything that needs branch manager attention

That's it. Not "what I'm working on today," not "what I worked on yesterday in detail." If a desk tries to explain the history of a problem, the facilitator redirects: *"Let's take that offline — action item: who owns it, by when?"*

## What the facilitator does

The facilitator (KAM or branch manager, rotating is fine) owns three things:

**1. Timekeeping.** 15 minutes means 15 minutes. Set a visible timer.

**2. Capturing MoM in real time.** Someone is typing into the Daily MoM Template as the conversation happens. If nobody's capturing, the huddle might as well not exist — it'll all evaporate.

**3. Forcing clarity on action items.** Every action gets: one owner, one deadline. Not "the team will look into it." If someone volunteers, great. If nobody volunteers, facilitator assigns.

## What goes where

```
Productivity data (file counts)   →  Master sheet
Today's discussion / blockers     →  Daily MoM (new copy of template)
Action items                      →  Daily MoM "Action Items" section
Escalations (for branch manager)  →  Daily MoM "Escalations" section
Account-level commitments         →  AM Tracker (not the huddle)
Shipment status                   →  DSR (not the huddle)
```

The huddle is for **today's operational reality**. If something is bigger than today, it belongs in one of the other two tools.

## Anti-patterns — things that kill huddles

**The 30-minute monologue.** One person, usually senior, explains something in detail while everyone else stands there. Fix: facilitator says "let's take that offline" and moves on.

**The silent huddle.** Everyone says "nothing to report" because speaking up gets you assigned more work. Fix: branch manager has to actively notice this pattern and make it safe to raise problems.

**The meeting about the meeting.** "Did we cover yesterday's action items? Who's following up on the Tuesday discussion?" If actions from two days ago matter, they're on a tracker. The huddle is about today.

**Remote-first huddles with no camera.** Voice-only standups become tacitly optional — people mute, do other work, drift. Fix: require cameras on for remote attendees, or hold the huddle in person and let remote folks dial in as observers.

**No rotation of facilitation.** If the branch manager always runs it, the huddle becomes about what the branch manager wants to hear. Rotate weekly.

## Using the Master sheet productively

The Master sheet is not a performance review tool. Do not use it to rank desks or embarrass anyone. Its real purpose is capacity visibility — noticing that:

- Desk 3 did 6 files a day for a week, then suddenly 0 → something's wrong, check in
- Desk 1 is consistently low → is there a workload distribution issue?
- Monthly total jumped 40% → who's going to break first, or do we need hiring?

The monthly pivot at the bottom of the Master sheet tells you the story over time. Look at it once a month with the team. Use it to start conversations, not end them.

## What the MoM sheets buy you six months later

The Daily MoM sheets, kept over time, become an unreasonably valuable archive.

- A new branch manager joins → reads six months of MoMs, knows the branch in a week
- A customer complaint escalates ("you promised us X in November") → search the MoMs, find the exact meeting, know the truth
- An internal debate about whether a process change worked → compare MoMs from before and after

The archive is worth more than any individual huddle. That's why the documentation discipline matters more than the meeting itself.

## Starting from scratch — the first two weeks

If the branch has never done huddles before, the first two weeks will feel awkward.

- **Days 1–3:** People show up, don't know what to say, meeting ends early. That's fine.
- **Days 4–7:** Someone raises a real blocker. The huddle resolves it faster than email would have. Others notice.
- **Week 2:** Patterns emerge. Someone starts bringing up recurring issues. The MoM archive starts being useful.
- **Week 3+:** The huddle is self-sustaining. Now the discipline is keeping it from drifting past 15 minutes.

The only thing that fails here is abandoning too early. If the branch manager skips two huddles in the first month, the team correctly concludes it's not a priority and stops showing up.
