# Account Management Playbook

How to run a phased account plan that actually gets executed.

The AM Tracker is just a spreadsheet. The playbook is what makes it work. This guide covers the operating rhythm around the tracker — when to update it, how to use the phases, and the mistakes to avoid.

---

## The three phases, in detail

### Phase I — Foundation (first 30–60 days)

**Purpose:** Build the scaffolding the account needs before any serious volume flows.

Typical Phase I tasks:
- **SOP creation** — A written document that defines how the forwarder will handle the account's freight. Covers booking flow, documentation, exception handling, billing, communication cadence. Signed off by both sides.
- **Contact matrix** — A single page listing who on both sides does what. Who approves bookings? Who handles exceptions? Who signs invoices? Who attends QBRs? This prevents the perpetual "who do I call about X?" problem.
- **Pricing templates / rate cards** — Agreed rate structures for the core lanes. Versioned (v1.0, v1.1, etc.). Published centrally.
- **Introductory QBR** — Not a review (there's nothing to review yet), but a formal kickoff with both sides' leadership to align on what success looks like.
- **Credit terms & billing setup** — Finance side. Often forgotten, often the first thing that causes friction.

Phase I is done when the customer can send a booking and the forwarder can execute it end-to-end without any ad-hoc decisions.

### Phase II — Activation (months 2–4)

**Purpose:** Execute the first shipments and refine the scaffolding based on what actually happens.

Typical Phase II tasks:
- **First 10 shipments — close tracking** — Every shipment in this window gets extra attention. Issues get logged, even small ones. Patterns emerge.
- **Exception handling playbook** — Derived from the issues seen in the first 10 shipments. What went wrong? What should happen next time? Documented.
- **DSR format finalization** — The DSR template should have been set up in Phase I; by now the customer has seen it daily and has opinions. Incorporate feedback, lock the format.
- **First performance review** — Formal review at ~60 days. Data-driven: OTIF%, average transit time, exception rate, invoicing accuracy.

Phase II is done when the account runs on its own for a week without daily intervention from the KAM.

### Phase III — Growth (month 4+)

**Purpose:** Expand volume, lanes, and services.

Typical Phase III tasks:
- **Lane expansion** — Move from core lanes into adjacent ones.
- **Cross-sell / upsell** — Air freight customer adds sea freight, sea customer adds customs brokerage, etc.
- **Quarterly QBRs** — Now with real performance data to discuss.
- **Contract renewal / commercial deepening** — Moving from transactional to contractual relationships.

Phase III never really ends; it's the steady state of a healthy account.

## Operating cadence

**Weekly** — Update the tracker. Move tasks between statuses. Add new tasks that came up. This is a 15-minute job if you do it weekly; a 90-minute job if you let it slip for a month.

**Monthly** — Review the tracker with the account team. Look at the Summary sheet: is the ratio of Completed / In Progress / Pending / Blocked healthy?

**Quarterly** — Review with the customer during QBR. The tracker is a natural artefact to share — it shows you're thoughtful about the account. (Caveat: only share sections that are customer-relevant. Internal bits like "discuss discount strategy with pricing" stay internal.)

---

## Status definitions — strictness matters

The whole tracker falls apart if statuses get used sloppily. Strict definitions:

- **Completed** — Delivered, signed off, no further action. Not "mostly done" — actually done.
- **In Progress** — Work has started, owner is actively working on it this week. If nobody's touched it in two weeks, it's not In Progress, it's Pending.
- **Pending** — Not yet started. Waiting on prerequisite, decision, or capacity. A task sitting in Pending for a month is a warning sign that belongs in the monthly review.
- **Blocked** — Cannot proceed due to external dependency. The distinguishing feature vs Pending: Blocked requires escalation to resolve; Pending just requires time.

The bias should be toward **fewer tasks, better tracked**, not many tasks poorly tracked. If a Phase has more than ~15 open tasks, consolidate.

---

## Common mistakes to avoid

**1. Using the tracker as a dumping ground.** Every stray thought gets added. The tracker balloons, nothing gets done, everyone stops trusting it. Rule: if a task isn't going to get started in the next 30 days, don't add it — put it in a parking lot doc instead.

**2. No owner, or "the team" as owner.** Tasks without a named individual owner get orphaned. Always one name, always a person, never a team.

**3. Dates that never update.** If "Date to End" slips by more than a week, change it with a note explaining why, don't just leave it stale. A stale end date is a lie the tracker tells the team.

**4. Treating the tracker as confidential.** The account team should see it. In many cases the customer should see a filtered version. Trackers that live in one KAM's drive are single points of failure.

**5. Skipping the Summary sheet.** The summary is where you notice that 60% of Phase I tasks are Pending four weeks in. That's a conversation to have, not a metric to hide.

## Adapting for different account sizes

**Small accounts (low volume, transactional)** — Use Phase I only. Skip the tracker past Phase I.

**Mid-size accounts (steady volume, multiple lanes)** — Full Phase I and II, light Phase III.

**Strategic accounts (major volume, enterprise, QBRs)** — Full three-phase model. The tracker is shared with the customer quarterly.

**Brand-new accounts with no history** — Start with Phase I. Don't skip ahead even if the account is big.
